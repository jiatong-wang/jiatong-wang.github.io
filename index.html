About

I am a junior neuroscience researcher with a background in both Biology and Medicine and a strong focus on neural circuit mechanisms underlying visceral sensation and autonomic regulation. My work integrates anatomy, physiology, and advanced imaging approaches to investigate how peripheral signals — particularly from the digestive system - are encoded and processed within the central nervous system (brainstem, etc).

I am currently seeking opportunities to further develop my training in circuit neuroscience, with the long-term goal of contributing to a deeper mechanistic understanding of interoception and its role in health and disease.

Research

Current & Past Projects

01

MS Thesis · 2025–Present

Anatomical and Functional Organization of Brainstem Circuits Regulating Gastric Motility

How does the brain control the rhythmic, coordinated movements of the stomach to facilitate digestion or trigger vomiting? I use viral tracing, neuronal labeling, and fiber photometry to investigate the connectivity between brainstem and gastric motor neurons, as well as their activity dynamics during digestion.

02

Undergraduate Research · 2023–2025

Stomach Mechanosensation in Health and Disease

How does the nervous system sense gastric distention and respond to it? Is this sensing altered in individuals with obesity? I combined viral tracing and in vivo fluorescent imaging to map the distribution of mechanosensory nerve endings in the gastrointestinal tract and to examine their roles in digestion. Using RNA sequencing, I have also begun to investigate changes in the nodose ganglion under diet-induced obesity.

03

Undergraduate Research · 2024–2025

Vagus-Mediated Inflammatory Sensation in the Liver

Why do bacterial infections (e.g., the common cold or gastroenteritis) lead to a loss of appetite? Using RNA fluorescence in situ hybridization (RNA FISH), I examined the expression of inflammation-related receptors in the nodose ganglion, and performed ex vivo calcium imaging for functional validation. With conditional knockout models, I further assessed behavioral phenotypes, including feeding, locomotion, and sleep. This project was led by Dr. Na Zhang.

04

Research Interest · Future PhD

Interoception and More

With the growing momentum of research, our understanding of interoception—across systems such as the gastrointestinal tract, circulation, and urinary system—has advanced substantially. However, how does the brain integrate these signals and efficiently coordinate internal needs, external environmental demands, and energy expenditure? Elucidating these mechanisms will deepen our understanding of ourselves and may open new avenues for tackling metabolic disorders.
